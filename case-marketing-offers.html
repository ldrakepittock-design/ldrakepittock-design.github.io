Service Design Facilitation Banking · KeyBank

How might we simplify marketing offers?

KeyBank's marketing premium fulfillment process had been built over decades on legacy systems — no one person or team understood the whole thing. I untangled it through service blueprinting, stakeholder workshops, and careful facilitation, generating 140+ ideas and the org-wide alignment needed to act on them.

Context

A process no one fully understood — least of all the people running it

KeyBank's Marketing Premium Fulfillment (MPF) process — the system that pays out marketing bonuses when clients open accounts tied to promotional offers — had evolved organically over decades. It existed across 8 variations, touched dozens of teams, and ran on legacy technology that had been patched and workaround-ed into a barely functional state.

Critically: the people running the process worked in silos. No one had ever seen the whole thing. That invisibility was the root cause of nearly every problem.

Role: Project Lead
Process

Making the system visible before trying to fix it

01 Offer Mapping Co-diagrammed all existing offer types with stakeholders to create a shared taxonomy
02 Focus Groups Small group sessions to draft initial service blueprints for each of the 5 core processes
03 Blueprint Validation Cross-functional workshop to validate blueprints and surface pain points
04 Ideation Generative ideation prompts per pain point cluster, generating 140+ ideas in small groups
05 Prioritization Effort/impact matrix voting to identify the most actionable ideas
06 Implementation Plans Assigned prioritized ideas to project owners with clear dependencies and next steps
Service Design

Five blueprints. One shared reality.

The service blueprints were the pivotal deliverable. Across five process variants (in-branch with targeted offer, in-branch with event-based offer, in-branch with mass market offer, online with mass/targeted offer, and the exceptions process), I mapped the full system: customer actions, frontstage actions, backstage actions, technology touchpoints, and known failure points.

Seeing these blueprints for the first time was revelatory for many stakeholders. One leader noted that the workshop "opened everyone's eyes to everyone else's challenges because they typically just work in their silo."

The most common source of errors wasn't any team's incompetence — it was the handoffs between teams that nobody had ever formally mapped. Fixing the handoffs was more valuable than optimizing individual steps.

Ideation & Prioritization

140+ ideas, focused down to what matters most

Using generative ideation prompts tied directly to the pain points surfaced by the blueprints, small cross-functional groups produced over 140 ideas across two days. Rather than letting the group lose steam in open voting, I structured a two-step prioritization: an effort/impact matrix plotting all ideas, followed by dot voting on the low-effort, high-impact quadrant.

This gave teams a clear mandate: here are the ideas most worth pursuing, here's who owns them, and here's what needs to happen first.

Prioritized ideas were assigned to owners, implementation plans were drafted, and leaders had a roadmap for the first time.

Reflection

What I'd do differently

The co-diagramming process for mapping offer types was unexpectedly difficult — stakeholders disagreed about basic definitions that seemed obvious to each individual team. In future projects like this, I'd budget significantly more time for the taxonomy phase before any blueprinting begins. Shared language is a prerequisite for shared understanding.

I'd also push harder for a follow-up session 60 days post-workshop to review progress on the prioritized ideas. The handoff to project owners worked well initially, but without a structured check-in, momentum can stall as day-to-day priorities take over.
