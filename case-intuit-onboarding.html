Service Design Journey Mapping FinTech · Intuit (via Akraya)

How might we transform the onboarding experience?

Intuit's customer onboarding process was slow, inconsistent, and invisible to most of the teams involved in delivering it. I led service design research and blueprinting work to map the reality, surface friction, and build a scalable path forward.

Context

An onboarding experience no one owned end-to-end

Intuit's customer onboarding involved multiple teams — product, sales, engineering, and operations — each responsible for their own piece, but with no shared view of the full journey. Customers experienced delays, confusion, and dropped handoffs. Internally, teams were solving for their slice without visibility into the broader problem.

My role was to bring the whole system into view: research the current experience, map it in a way that surfaced cross-team dependencies, and facilitate alignment on a better future state.

Role: Senior Service Designer
Process

From fragmented pieces to a coherent system

01 Discovery Research 30+ interviews with stakeholders and frontline employees to understand the lived experience of onboarding
02 Journey Mapping Current-state maps tracking emotional and operational friction across all customer touchpoints
03 Service Blueprinting Blueprints connecting people, processes, tools, and product systems to expose cross-team dependencies
04 Co-Design Workshops Cross-functional sessions with product, design, sales, and operations to envision the future state
05 Design Principles Lightweight principles and templates to embed journey-based thinking across teams
Research

Surfacing what the data couldn't show

The quantitative data showed onboarding took 30+ days on average — but it couldn't explain why, or where customers felt most lost. Through interviews with frontline employees and stakeholders, I was able to identify the specific moments where handoffs broke down, where systems didn't talk to each other, and where customers were left without clear next steps.

The biggest source of delay wasn't any single team's failure — it was the gaps between teams. No one had a shared view of what "done" looked like at each stage, so handoffs were unclear and customers fell through the cracks.

Service Design

Making the invisible visible

The service blueprints were the central deliverable that changed how teams saw the problem. For the first time, product, sales, engineering, and ops could see their work alongside each other — and understand how their decisions downstream affected other teams and customers.

I also built reusable frameworks and lightweight templates (leveraging AI tools including Cursor) to make journey-based thinking scalable across the organization, not dependent on a service designer being in the room.

Reflection

What I'd do differently

Service blueprinting is a powerful tool for creating organizational alignment — but it can become unwieldy if it tries to capture everything at once. In this project, I learned to scope blueprints to specific journeys rather than the entire onboarding system, which made them more actionable in workshop settings.

I'd also invest earlier in building internal champions who can carry the journey-based thinking forward. Templates and frameworks help, but the mindset shift requires people inside the organization who believe in the approach and can advocate for it when I'm not in the room.
