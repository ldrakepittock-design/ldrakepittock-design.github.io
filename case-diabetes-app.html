How might we engage people with diabetes?

Context

Building for people who don't want to be reminded they're sick

A major diabetes device manufacturer wanted to create an app to engage people with diabetes (PWDs) on a daily basis — with the hope of becoming a trusted part of their health routine. The challenge: existing diabetes apps had poor engagement and high churn.

As Content Contributor, I focused on the research, insight development, and strategic recommendations — working alongside a team of designers who shaped the concept.

Research-driven product strategy

Key Insight

Shame, not guilt — a distinction that changed everything

One of the most striking moments of the research: when we conducted the study as a general "health and wellness" study (without mentioning diabetes), participants didn't bring up diabetes until directly asked. They talked about their sleep, exercise, stress — and avoided the topic entirely.

People with diabetes feel shame — not guilt — about their condition. Guilt says "I did something bad." Shame says "I am something bad." Diabetes apps that lead with diabetes reinforce that shame. The product had to work with their identity, not against it.

A second key finding: 69% of apps used by PWDs to manage their health are wellness apps — not diabetes apps. People were already solving for this, just without a diabetes-specific product. This revealed both the problem and the opportunity.

Strategy

Reframe: from diabetes management to metabolic health

The research led to a fundamental strategic pivot. Instead of building a diabetes app, we recommended building a metabolic health app — one that could appeal to PWDs without triggering shame, while simultaneously opening the product up to the vastly larger pre-diabetes and metabolic health market.

This reframe expanded the addressable market from ~6M insulin-treated Type 2 patients to nearly 300M people with some connection to metabolic health — a 50x expansion of potential reach.

Concept

A wellness app that helps you find habits that work for you

The resulting concept was a metabolic wellness app built around four core features: Track (health metrics that matter most to you), Insights (understand what's affecting your body), Explore (tips and reflections from people like you), and Try (experiment with new habits safely). Five design attributes — Simple, Empowering, Joyful, Safe, Fulfilling — guided every design decision.

Reflection

What I'd do differently

The decision to blind participants on the research topic was one of the most valuable methodological choices on this project — and one I'd replicate on any health or sensitive-topic research. It removed the social desirability bias that would have colored every response if participants knew we were studying diabetes specifically.

If I were running this again, I'd push for more participatory methods earlier — co-design sessions with PWDs, not just interviews. The diary study gave us rich behavioral data, but co-creation would have allowed participants to help us design solutions, not just surface problems.
